# YouTube Data API Validation

## Purpose

vidIQ indicated demand. The YouTube Data API connection was then used to test whether real people were watching recent videos about each candidate. Searches generally used a January 1, 2025 publication floor, U.S. region context and view-count ordering. A second API call retrieved video statistics and duration by ID.

Counts below are point-in-time snapshots from 2026-07-12. Direct URLs and exact metadata are in [SOURCE_LOG.md](SOURCE_LOG.md).

### What this validates

- Demand is distributed across multiple creators, vehicles and angles.
- Long-form durations around 10–15 minutes are accepted by the audience.
- Comments are substantial enough to support a question-led content backlog.
- The audience watches both broad decision videos and model/ownership stories.

### What this does not validate

- It does not prove the proposed channel will receive similar views.
- Search ordered by view count favors winners and does not reveal the full failure rate.
- Personal ownership stories may enjoy trust advantages that an asset-based channel must replace with transparent evidence.

The blueprint therefore uses these videos as proof of active viewing—not as a revenue forecast.

## Cross-candidate evidence

### Home Wi-Fi diagnosis

The five-video sample ranged from roughly 442K to 1.31M views. [W01–W05] The demand is excellent. However, the results mixed broad entertainment-led technology channels, hardware optimization and narrow operating-system fixes. The likely channel would attract one-off support searches more easily than a coherent subscriber identity.

### Scam prevention

Broad keyword demand was strong in vidIQ, and channel discovery found breakout faceless examples. The generic English educational search sample was less compelling than those channel results. This inconsistency, plus the dependence of some winners on third-party scam clips, lowered the score.

### Sleeper train / rail planning

The sample ranged from roughly 177K to 612K views. [R01–R04] Demand is real, but the strongest rail channels and videos usually rely on original travel footage. This directly conflicts with the asset-only guardrail.

### Appliance repair

The sample ranged from roughly 684K to 2.90M views. [A01–A05] This is powerful demand proof. It simultaneously proves that the winning product is a visible repair demonstration. An asset-only substitute would be less useful and could introduce safety problems.

### Megaproject explainers

The sample ranged from roughly 862K to 3.24M views. [M01–M05] The audience is enormous. So are the animation, research, rights-clearance and storytelling expectations. This is a high-production tournament, not an attainable low-footage opportunity.

### Airport navigation

The sample ranged from roughly 148K to 1.18M views. [P01–P05] Travelers actively seek arrival, transfer and transit walkthroughs. The strongest content is location-current and footage-rich, making accuracy maintenance and guardrail compliance difficult.

### HVAC/home energy

The sample ranged from roughly 245K to 793K views. [H01–H04] Demand and advertiser value are strong. Safety, licensing, diagnostic equipment and custom filming reduced feasibility.

### Used-device diagnostics

The sample ranged from roughly 250K to 887K views. [T01–T05] The audience is strong, but query drift into phones and international markets suggests a narrower U.S. laptop concept would require more validation. Hardware access is also a recurring credibility cost.

### Subscription traps/dark patterns

The five recent English long-form examples returned only 153–966 views. [D01–D05] That conflicts with attractive vidIQ keyword numbers. The candidate was rejected because actual recent viewing in the sampled result set did not align.

## API-derived packaging lessons

1. **Decision language beats taxonomy.** The successful used-EV titles ask whether the purchase is worth it or warn about a mistake.
2. **A named vehicle broadens the emotional hook.** Tesla, Taycan and Ioniq 5 provide a concrete object while the lesson generalizes.
3. **Ten to fifteen minutes is a credible launch range.** It is long enough for an evidence chain and short enough to avoid documentary-level production.
4. **Depreciation is both fear and opportunity.** Buyers want to know whether falling prices create a bargain or a trap.
5. **Ownership experience is the incumbent advantage.** EV Second Opinion must counter it with a visible, repeatable research method, not anonymous assertions.

## Planned API workflow for each future episode

Before greenlighting a topic:

1. Search the exact problem and model-year cluster.
2. Pull the top 20 relevant videos from the last 24 months.
3. Remove irrelevant language/region and query-drift results.
4. Calculate median views, median age, channel-size spread and recent upload frequency.
5. Read titles, descriptions and comments for unresolved questions without copying claims.
6. Log the IDs and timestamp in the episode source sheet.
7. Reject topics where only one outlier carries the demand signal.

## Minimum greenlight rule

A planned evergreen video is greenlit when at least one of these conditions is met:

- three relevant videos from different channels exceeded 50,000 views in the previous 24 months; or
- a precise search query has a high-intent vidIQ score and at least five relevant recent videos show steady, non-zero viewing; or
- a current official change creates urgent buyer impact, such as the used clean vehicle credit ending. [S01]

The rule can be overridden only in the episode brief with a written reason, such as completing a strategic playlist or answering a repeated viewer question.
