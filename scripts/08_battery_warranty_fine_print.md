# Script 08 — EV Battery Warranty Fine Print: What 70% Really Means

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Battery Truth  
**Thumbnail:** `70% WARRANTY`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — battery gauge falls from 100% to 70%. Giant text: “COVERED?”]**

**NARRATOR:** A used EV listing says the battery is covered for eight years or one hundred thousand miles with seventy-percent capacity retention.

Many buyers hear: “If the battery gets weak, I get a new one.”

That is not necessarily what the warranty says.

A battery warranty is a contract with clocks, thresholds, exclusions and a claim process. Today we will turn the headline into six questions you need answered for the exact car.

**[TITLE: THE SIX-PART BATTERY WARRANTY FILE]**

### 1. Which warranty document controls?

**[VISUAL: current web page versus vehicle-specific warranty booklet.]**

Start with the document tied to the vehicle’s original sale, model year and region.

Tesla’s current support page lists battery and drive-unit coverage for Model 3 and Model Y variants, including eight-year mileage limits and minimum seventy-percent capacity retention. The same page warns that its summary is for general knowledge and that the warranty supplied with the vehicle is authoritative. [S06]

That caveat matters for every brand. A current website may describe cars sold today, not the used vehicle in front of you.

Ask for:

- warranty booklet or digital document for the vehicle;
- original in-service date;
- exact trim and battery variant;
- region where the car was first sold;
- evidence that coverage transfers to the next owner.

### 2. Which clock ends first?

**[VISUAL: calendar clock races odometer clock.]**

“Eight years or one hundred thousand miles” means whichever limit arrives first.

Calculate both:

- calendar time remaining from the in-service date;
- mileage remaining from the odometer.

A high-mileage young car may have little mileage protection left. A low-mileage older car may be near the date limit.

Record the answer as an exact date and odometer limit, not “about three years.”

### 3. What does the capacity threshold mean?

**[VISUAL: 100 kWh illustrative original usable capacity → 70 kWh threshold. Label: illustration only.]**

A seventy-percent threshold generally means the warranty’s capacity remedy can be triggered only when the manufacturer’s approved measurement determines that capacity has fallen below the stated threshold during the coverage period, subject to the contract.

It does not automatically mean:

- the battery is considered defective at seventy-nine percent;
- the car has seventy percent of its original EPA range;
- a dashboard estimate is the accepted measurement;
- replacement means a brand-new pack at one hundred percent;
- every range complaint is covered.

Recurrent notes that range availability is not the same as degradation. [S09] That is why the manufacturer’s measurement method matters.

### 4. What remedy is promised?

**[VISUAL: repair, module replacement, refurbished/replacement assembly, restore to threshold.]**

Read the remedy language.

The manufacturer may reserve the right to repair, replace modules, install a remanufactured component or restore capacity to at least a specified level. Do not assume the remedy is a new full-capacity pack unless the contract says so.

Ask:

- Who chooses repair versus replacement?
- Can replacement components be refurbished?
- What capacity must the remedy achieve?
- Does the repaired/replaced part receive a new warranty or only the remaining original term?
- Are towing or transportation costs covered?

Do not invent the answer from forum posts. Extract it from the controlling document.

### 5. What is excluded?

**[VISUAL: exclusion cards: damage, misuse, unsupported equipment, failure to follow instructions.]**

Warranty exclusions vary. Common categories can include collision or external damage, misuse, unauthorized modification, environmental events or damage associated with unsupported equipment.

Tesla’s current page specifically says its limited warranty does not cover damage caused by third-party bidirectional adapters or chargers. [S06]

For the vehicle you are considering, read exclusions involving:

- charging equipment and adapters;
- salvage or branded title;
- collision or underbody damage;
- commercial use;
- modifications;
- required software or service actions;
- transfer and geographic restrictions.

An exclusion is not proof that a claim will be denied. It is a risk that needs to be understood before purchase.

### 6. How is a claim measured and submitted?

**[VISUAL: symptom → service appointment → approved test → result → remedy decision.]**

A buyer needs the process, not just the threshold.

Find out:

- which warning, symptom or capacity result starts the process;
- whether the vehicle must be taken to an authorized service center;
- which test the manufacturer uses;
- whether software must be current;
- what records are required;
- how disputes are handled;
- whether the manufacturer can perform remote diagnostics.

Tesla’s current support page says the Service team can review appointment details and perform remote diagnostics before an in-person visit, while some repairs require inspection before coverage is confirmed. [S06]

Again, verify the actual process for the exact brand and vehicle.

### What seventy percent means for the buyer

**[VISUAL: use-case route versus remaining range.]**

A battery can be above the warranty threshold and still be a poor fit for your route. It can also be below its new-car capability and remain perfectly useful for a short commute.

The Department of Energy recommends matching range and charging to daily mileage, longer routes, climate, hills and loads. [S02]

So use two separate tests:

- **Contract test:** would the manufacturer’s approved measurement and conditions qualify for a remedy?
- **Buyer-fit test:** does the vehicle’s conservative remaining capability serve your life?

Do not confuse them.

### The warranty extraction sheet

**[ON SCREEN: fillable table.]**

Write down:

1. controlling document and version;
2. in-service date;
3. date limit;
4. mileage limit;
5. capacity threshold;
6. measurement method;
7. remedy options;
8. exclusions;
9. transfer status;
10. claim contact and records.

If the seller cannot help with the vehicle-specific warranty, ask the manufacturer using the VIN before you price the protection.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: WARRANTY = FLOOR, NOT NEW-CAR PROMISE.]**

A battery warranty is valuable when it is active, transferable, clearly documented and meaningful for the risk you are accepting.

**Buy candidate:** the clocks and terms are verified, the battery evidence is consistent and the car fits even above the warranty threshold.

**Wait or negotiate:** coverage is close to expiring, the controlling document is missing or the seller’s promise is broader than the contract.

**Walk or price as uninsured risk:** coverage is expired, non-transferable or materially excluded—and the price does not compensate.

Seventy percent is not a prediction and not a definition of a dead battery. It is a contractual threshold that must be read in context.
