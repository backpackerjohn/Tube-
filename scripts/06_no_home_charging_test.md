# Script 06 — Can You Own an EV Without Home Charging? Run This Test

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Charging Reality  
**Thumbnail:** `NO HOME CHARGER?`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — apartment building, street parking and an EV listing. Question appears: “GOOD DEAL?” Then: “WHERE WILL IT CHARGE?”]**

**NARRATOR:** You found the right used EV at the right price. There is one problem: you cannot charge at home.

That does not automatically rule out an EV. It does mean the buying test changes.

The Department of Energy says buyers should start with daily mileage, charging options and compatible public stations for longer routes. [S02] Without home charging, availability, time, price and backup become part of the vehicle itself.

Run this five-minute test before you schedule a test drive.

**[TITLE: THE NO-HOME-CHARGING TEST]**

### Step 1 — Build your energy week

**[VISUAL: seven-day calendar; miles entered.]**

Write down a normal week of driving—not your best week and not a rare vacation.

For each day:

- miles driven;
- where the car parks for at least one hour;
- whether a compatible charger is available there;
- longest routine day;
- emergency or unplanned mileage you need to absorb.

Convert miles into an energy estimate using a conservative efficiency for the exact vehicle and season. Label the estimate; do not pretend it is a guarantee.

If your week requires, for example, sixty kilowatt-hours, the question becomes: can your real parking schedule reliably replace sixty kilowatt-hours without controlling a home charger?

### Step 2 — Find anchor charging, not occasional charging

**[VISUAL: three candidates—workplace, grocery, gym. One marked ANCHOR.]**

An anchor charger is somewhere you already park long enough, often enough, at a predictable price.

Good anchors can include:

- workplace charging you are authorized to use;
- a residential garage or lot near home with dependable access;
- a regular destination such as a gym or shopping center where parking and charger rules permit routine use;
- a public fast-charging site on an existing commute, not a special trip.

A charger shown on a map is not automatically an anchor. Verify:

- connector compatibility;
- hours and access;
- parking fees;
- idle fees;
- network account requirements;
- realistic availability during your parking window;
- backup sites.

### Step 3 — Price the public-only reality

**[VISUAL: cost per kWh + session fee + parking + detour time.]**

Calculate your expected monthly charging cost at the stations you will actually use.

Include:

- energy price;
- network membership;
- session or parking fees;
- detour miles;
- time spent waiting or relocating the car.

Compare that with the gasoline or hybrid alternative using the same annual mileage. Do not use a cheap home-electricity rate you cannot access.

The IEA says EVs often have lower lifetime total cost because of fuel and maintenance savings, but the result varies with purchase price and operating conditions. [S08] Public-only charging can narrow or reverse part of the energy advantage.

### Step 4 — Run the disruption test

**[VISUAL: charger occupied, broken, snowstorm, job change.]**

Ask four uncomfortable questions:

1. What do I do if the anchor charger is unavailable for three days?
2. Can I reach a second compatible site without arriving nearly empty?
3. What happens if my workplace changes its charging policy or I change jobs?
4. What happens in the worst common weather when range falls and charging may take longer?

The DOE advises leaving extra range buffer for hills, heavy loads and heating or air-conditioning use. [S02]

A plan that works only in ideal weather and perfect charger availability is not robust enough for a purchase.

### Step 5 — Match the car to the charging life

**[VISUAL: three vehicles, different range/charging speed/price.]**

Without home charging, the “best” used EV may change.

A vehicle with a lower price but slow fast charging may demand too much time. A longer-range vehicle may reduce charging frequency. A smaller battery may refill quickly enough during workplace parking. A highly efficient car can stretch each public kilowatt-hour.

Compare:

- conservative usable range;
- charging speed over your typical stop window;
- network/connector fit;
- efficiency;
- purchase-price premium for more range;
- remaining warranty and battery evidence.

Do not pay for maximum range automatically. Pay for the least expensive configuration that passes your real energy week with a backup.

### The score

**[SCORECARD: 0–10.]**

Give yourself two points for each:

- dependable anchor location;
- monthly cost is acceptable;
- backup location exists;
- normal week requires no special charging trip;
- worst-common-week buffer works.

**8–10 points:** viable candidate. Continue to battery, warranty and VIN checks.

**5–7 points:** conditional. Test the routine for two weeks using the charger network before buying, where practical; price your time honestly.

**0–4 points:** wait. A hybrid or gasoline vehicle may fit better today, or the EV decision should wait until charging access improves.

This score is an editorial tool, not a validated scientific scale.

### Two use cases

**[VISUAL: illustrative examples.]**

**Case A:** A renter drives twenty miles per day, has reliable workplace charging three days a week and two public backups. The EV can fit even without home charging.

**Case B:** A street parker drives eighty miles daily, has one busy fast charger six miles off route and pays a high public rate. A cheap used EV may create a recurring time and cost problem.

These examples illustrate the test; they do not claim that every renter or commuter matches them.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: PARKING FIRST. CAR SECOND.]**

You can own an EV without home charging when:

- charging is attached to places you already park;
- the exact car is compatible;
- expected cost and time are acceptable;
- a backup exists;
- the plan survives common disruptions.

Wait or choose another powertrain when the plan depends on one station, frequent special trips or a price advantage that disappears at public charging rates.

**[CHECKLIST]**

1. Map energy week.
2. Verify anchor charger.
3. Calculate actual public cost.
4. Test disruption.
5. Choose the car for that system.

**[END SCREEN: PRICE DROP — WHY.]**

If charging works, the next question is price. A used EV can lose value quickly. Is that your bargain—or a warning? That is next.
