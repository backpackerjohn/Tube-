# Script 05 — The Charging Compatibility Check Every Used EV Needs

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Charging Reality  
**Thumbnail:** `WILL IT CHARGE?`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — an attractive used EV sits between three charger icons. Two turn red.]**

**NARRATOR:** A used EV can have enough range, a healthy battery and a great price—and still be the wrong car because it does not charge where, when or how you need it.

“Can it plug in?” is not the compatibility test.

The real test has five layers: location, connector, speed, price and backup.

**[TITLE: THE FIVE-LAYER CHARGING CHECK]**

### Layer 1 — Location: where will most charging actually happen?

**[VISUAL: weekly calendar with home, work, grocery and highway.]**

The Department of Energy recommends starting with daily driving and where charging is available—at home, work and public locations—then checking compatible stations for longer trips. [S02]

Map a normal seven-day week.

- Miles driven each day.
- Parking duration at home and work.
- Reliable charging within walking distance.
- Longest routine day.
- Common weekend or family route.

Do not begin with the car. Begin with the parking.

If the car sits at home for ten hours with access to a suitable outlet or charger, a slower charge rate may be enough. If it parks on the street and depends on public charging, availability and price become central.

### Layer 2 — Connector: physical fit and network access

**[VISUAL: generic connector silhouettes with “vehicle inlet,” “station plug” and “adapter” labels.]**

Identify the exact vehicle inlet and the connectors used by the stations you plan to visit. Then check whether an adapter is supported for that vehicle, station and charging direction.

An adapter is not magic. Compatibility can depend on:

- AC versus DC charging;
- vehicle model and year;
- station/network support;
- adapter rating and certification;
- software enablement;
- manufacturer warranty terms.

Tesla’s warranty page, for example, states that damage caused by third-party vehicle adapters or chargers is not covered by its limited warranty. [S06] That does not make every third-party product unsafe; it means warranty and compatibility must be checked rather than assumed.

Never use a homemade or uncertified high-power charging solution.

### Layer 3 — Speed: what the car can accept and what the station can deliver

**[VISUAL: three bottlenecks — car, charger, temperature/battery state.]**

The number printed on a charging station is not a promise that your car will receive that power.

Charging speed is limited by the lowest active bottleneck:

- the vehicle’s onboard AC charger or DC fast-charge capability;
- the station’s available output;
- battery temperature and state of charge;
- shared power at the site;
- the vehicle’s charging curve and software.

For daily ownership, calculate time gained during the parking window, not a headline peak.

For road trips, compare realistic stop time over the useful charging band, not zero-to-one-hundred marketing.

**[ILLUSTRATIVE CALCULATION CARD]**

If a car adds roughly three kilowatt-hours during a short grocery stop, ask how many miles that represents at your realistic efficiency. The exact answer depends on the car and conditions; label all assumptions.

### Layer 4 — Price: home rate, public rate and time cost

**[VISUAL: three columns—home overnight, public Level 2, DC fast charging.]**

Charging cost can vary by location, network, subscription, session fee, parking fee and time of day.

Build three scenarios:

1. **Best normal case:** your usual home or work rate.
2. **Expected mixed case:** most charging at the usual location plus occasional public fast charging.
3. **Public-only case:** the realistic network and parking prices you would actually pay.

Do not compare a gasoline price with a national-average electricity price if you will mostly use expensive public charging.

The DOE provides a vehicle cost calculator and station tools, but your local utility and charging-network prices must be checked at production and purchase time. [S02]

### Layer 5 — Backup: what happens when Plan A fails?

**[VISUAL: preferred charger unavailable; route switches to alternatives.]**

A charging plan with one station is not a plan. It is a dependency.

For each important location or route, identify:

- preferred charger;
- second network or site;
- distance to backup;
- operating hours and access restrictions;
- recent reliability information if available;
- the minimum arrival buffer that keeps the backup reachable.

For home charging, the backup may be workplace charging or a nearby public site. For a road trip, it may be a slower station before the preferred stop.

### The compatibility worksheet

**[VISUAL: five rows score green/amber/red.]**

Score each layer:

- **Green:** verified and fits normal use.
- **Amber:** works with cost, time or reliability tradeoffs.
- **Red:** unsupported connector/adapter, insufficient energy during parking, unaffordable public-only plan or no safe backup.

A red connector or route gate overrides a high vehicle score. The car cannot compensate for infrastructure it cannot use.

### Seller questions

Before buying, ask:

1. Which charging cable and adapters are included?
2. Are they original, supported and undamaged?
3. Does the charge port operate normally on AC and, where practical, DC?
4. What charging limitations or service messages are present?
5. Are any charging-related repairs or campaigns documented?
6. Can the vehicle account or manual confirm compatibility?

A cable in the trunk has value only when it is correct, safe and useful for your setup.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD.]**

**Buy candidate:** the car can add enough energy during your normal parking, the connector path is supported, expected charging cost is acceptable and backup options exist.

**Wait or negotiate:** the car works only with an adapter or installation not yet verified, the public-only cost weakens the savings, or the backup route is thin.

**Walk for this use case:** the vehicle cannot use the needed stations, the required hardware is unsupported, or normal charging would consume more time and money than you accept.

A charging mismatch does not make the model bad. It makes the model wrong for this buyer.
