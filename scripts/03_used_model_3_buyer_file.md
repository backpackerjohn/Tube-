## Script

**[OPEN — three used Model 3 listings with similar prices. One has high mileage, one vague trim, one “full self-driving” claim.]**

**NARRATOR:** These three used Tesla Model 3 listings look almost interchangeable.

They are not.

The badge may be the same, but the battery, drivetrain, warranty clock, charging hardware, accident history, software features and seller evidence can change the value by thousands of dollars.

Recent buyer videos about older or used Teslas have attracted hundreds of thousands of views, and Reuters reported that the Model 3 is among the most widely available and sought-after used EVs in the United States. [Y01; Y02; S07]

That popularity creates choice. It also creates noise.

Here are the seven checks that decide whether a used Model 3 is a buy candidate, a negotiation or a walk.

**[TITLE: USED MODEL 3 — THE SEVEN-CHECK FILE]**

### Check 1 — Prove the configuration

**[VISUAL: listing title breaks into model year, trim, drivetrain, wheels, battery/warranty category.]**

Do not accept “Model 3 Long Range” as the complete identity.

Record:

- model year and build date when available;
- rear-wheel drive, all-wheel drive or Performance;
- wheel size;
- current odometer;
- exact advertised software features;
- original in-service date;
- whether the seller is Tesla, an independent dealer or a private party.

Why the wheels? Larger wheels can affect ride, tire cost and real-world efficiency. Why the seller type? Tesla’s current support page says pre-owned vehicles bought directly from Tesla receive the remainder of the original basic warranty, followed by an additional one year or ten thousand miles, while the balance of the original battery and drive-unit warranty still applies. That specific additional pre-owned coverage is tied to buying directly from Tesla; do not assume an independent listing includes it. [S06]

**[UNKNOWN CARD: exact battery variant may require vehicle-specific verification.]**

### Check 2 — Calculate every warranty clock

**[VISUAL: three clocks: basic, battery/drive unit, used-vehicle coverage.]**

Tesla’s current warranty page lists different battery and drive-unit mileage limits by Model 3 variant, all with an eight-year period and a minimum seventy-percent capacity-retention threshold. It also says the warranty provided with the specific vehicle is authoritative. [S06]

So do not ask only, “Is it under warranty?” Ask:

- Which warranty?
- Until what date?
- Until what mileage?
- What capacity threshold applies?
- Does it transfer?
- Was the car bought directly from Tesla, and does additional pre-owned coverage apply?

A four-year-old car with heavy mileage can have a very different risk profile from a six-year-old low-mileage car. Both clocks matter.

Screenshot or photograph the warranty page in the Tesla app when the owner can show it. Keep the vehicle-specific document, not a verbal promise.

### Check 3 — Build the battery evidence stack

**[VISUAL: pack identity, records, displayed range, efficiency, diagnostic test, use-case fit.]**

A full-charge estimate is not enough. Recurrent warns that range availability is not the same as battery degradation because software, efficiency and reserve capacity can influence the result. [S09]

For the Model 3, ask for:

- full-charge estimate with the state of charge visible;
- recent efficiency and trip data;
- charging or service records the owner is willing to provide;
- any battery or drive-unit replacement invoice;
- any manufacturer-supported health test available on that vehicle;
- a qualified pre-purchase inspection when the evidence is incomplete.

Then compare the conservative usable range with your longest routine trip and charging plan.

Do not claim a percentage of degradation from one dashboard screenshot.

### Check 4 — Run the VIN safety screen

**[VISUAL: NHTSA VIN lookup. Result panel with “scope limits” beside it.]**

Use NHTSA’s recall search on the exact VIN. NHTSA says the search can identify certain unrepaired recalls, but it may not show repaired recalls, some recent recalls, recalls more than fifteen years old or non-safety service campaigns. [S04]

Also inspect the service records and ask Tesla Service, where available to the owner, about open campaigns or concerns.

A clean NHTSA result means “no unrepaired recall shown within this tool’s scope.” It does not mean “this car has never had a problem.”

### Check 5 — Verify software and charging claims

**[VISUAL: seller claims on left; vehicle account/screen evidence on right.]**

Listings often use software language loosely.

Treat every paid-feature claim as unverified until the vehicle interface and account documentation show what is included and whether it transfers with the vehicle. Do not price the car based on a phrase such as “full self-driving capable” without understanding exactly what hardware, package and current entitlement are present.

For charging, verify:

- the connector and any adapter included;
- home-charging equipment included or excluded;
- compatibility with your usual public networks;
- route coverage and backup stations;
- whether the car’s charging performance matches your travel tolerance.

The Department of Energy recommends that buyers evaluate daily mileage, home/work charging and compatible stations along longer routes. [S02]

### Check 6 — Price the physical car, not the clean listing photos

**[VISUAL: tire tread, wheel damage, glass, panel gaps, underbody inspection, 12-volt/low-voltage system icon.]**

An asset-based channel cannot physically inspect your car, and that unknown must remain visible.

Use a qualified inspection and personally check ordinary used-car items:

- tire brand, size, matching set, tread and age;
- wheel damage;
- windshield and glass;
- body repair evidence and title history;
- suspension noises and alignment;
- climate control;
- charge-port operation;
- cameras, lights, doors, seats and screens;
- included charging cable and adapters.

Get an insurance quote before buying. A lower purchase price does not guarantee a lower local premium.

Do not work on or open high-voltage components.

### Check 7 — Compare capability per dollar

**[VISUAL: matrix with three listings and five columns.]**

Now put the listings in one table.

Columns:

- out-the-door price;
- conservative range fit;
- charging fit;
- warranty remaining;
- documented condition and unknowns.

Reuters reported rapid used-EV price normalization and strong used-EV sales growth. [S07] That creates bargains, but also means you should compare several cars instead of anchoring to original MSRP.

A slightly more expensive Model 3 may be the better value if it has documented coverage, compatible charging, better tires and clearer battery evidence. A cheap car with vague history is not discounted until the risk is priced.

### EV Second Opinion verdict

**[SCORECARD appears with gates.]**

A used Model 3 is a **buy candidate** when:

- configuration and included features are proven;
- warranty clocks are documented;
- battery clues agree and fit your routes;
- no unresolved safety gate appears;
- charging works for your actual life;
- condition and insurance are acceptable;
- the price compensates for remaining unknowns.

It is a **wait or negotiate** when the car may fit but the seller has not proven the trim, software, battery history or warranty. Your negotiation is the price of missing evidence.

It is a **walk** when the seller will not provide the VIN, material listing claims cannot be verified, an active fault or safety issue is unresolved, or the conservative charging/range scenario fails your routine.

**[ON SCREEN: Seven checks.]**

1. Configuration.
2. Warranty clocks.
3. Battery evidence.
4. VIN safety screen.
5. Software and charging claims.
6. Physical condition and insurance.
7. Capability per dollar.
