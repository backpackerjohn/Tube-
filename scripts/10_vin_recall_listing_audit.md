# Script 10 — The VIN and Recall Audit That Can Stop a Bad Used-EV Deal

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Listing Autopsy  
**Thumbnail:** `CHECK THIS VIN`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Production note

Use an owner-authorized VIN, a manufacturer demonstration VIN, or a visibly redacted example. Never expose private seller information. Re-check NHTSA’s current wording within 24 hours of upload.

## Script

**[OPEN — used-EV listing. The price and photos fade; the VIN field remains.]**

**NARRATOR:** A polished listing can tell you the color, mileage, monthly payment and how quickly the car supposedly will sell.

It may not tell you the one identifier that connects the vehicle to recalls, configuration, warranty and history: the VIN.

A VIN audit cannot prove that a used EV is good. It can stop you from treating an incomplete listing as evidence.

Today we are building a seven-step listing audit that ends with one of three results: proceed, investigate or walk.

**[TITLE: THE SEVEN-STEP VIN AUDIT]**

### Step 1 — Obtain and preserve the exact listing

**[VISUAL: dated capture with URL, seller, location, price, mileage and fees.]**

Save a dated copy of the listing before it changes.

Record:

- URL and access date;
- seller name and type;
- city and state;
- advertised price;
- mileage;
- model year and trim claim;
- title or accident statements;
- warranty claims;
- included software, cable or adapter claims;
- fees visible in the listing.

Do not publish the seller’s phone number, home address or other personal information. The purpose is to preserve claims for your own comparison, not to mobilize viewers against a seller.

### Step 2 — Match the VIN everywhere

**[VISUAL: VIN appears at windshield, door label, documents and digital record; mismatches turn red.]**

Ask for the full seventeen-character VIN before paying a deposit, subject to legitimate seller-security procedures. At an inspection, verify that the VIN matches across the visible vehicle labels, title/registration documents and the listing.

A mismatch can have an innocent explanation, such as a dealer template error. It still must be resolved before money changes hands.

Do not decode a VIN by eye and assume every option. Use reliable manufacturer or official data where available and confirm configuration from the vehicle itself.

### Step 3 — Run the NHTSA recall screen

**[SCREEN RECORDING: NHTSA VIN search. Result appears beside a limitation card.]**

NHTSA provides a VIN recall lookup for certain unrepaired safety recalls. The agency says the result does not necessarily include recalls that have already been repaired, some newly announced recalls, recalls older than fifteen years or non-safety service campaigns. [S04]

Read the result precisely.

If the tool shows an unrepaired safety recall:

- save the recall number and description;
- identify the stated risk and remedy;
- ask whether parts and service are available;
- confirm who will complete the remedy and when;
- do not assume a recall automatically makes the car worthless or safe to drive before remedy.

If the result shows no unrepaired recalls, say:

> “No unrepaired safety recall was shown in this lookup within the tool’s stated scope on this date.”

Do not say, “The car has a clean history.”

### Step 4 — Reconcile recall, service and replacement records

**[VISUAL: NHTSA result, invoices, manufacturer account and vehicle-history report converge.]**

Ask for service invoices and any manufacturer-account records the owner can lawfully share.

For an EV, look for documentation involving:

- traction-battery replacement or module work;
- drive-unit repair;
- charging-port or onboard-charger work;
- software or service campaigns;
- collision or underbody repair;
- low-voltage battery replacement;
- warranty claims;
- repeated unresolved faults.

A vehicle-history report can add information, but it is not guaranteed to contain every event. The goal is reconciliation: do independent records tell a consistent story?

A replacement battery is not automatically a negative. Record why it was replaced, when, at what mileage, what part was installed and what warranty applies afterward.

### Step 5 — Verify warranty claims against the exact car

**[VISUAL: listing says “battery warranty until 2031”; calendar and odometer calculate the claim.]**

Do not accept “battery warranty remaining” without calculating both clocks.

Tesla’s current warranty page, as one example, lists variant-specific Model 3 and Model Y battery and drive-unit coverage with a minimum seventy-percent capacity-retention threshold. It also says the warranty supplied with the vehicle is authoritative. [S06]

For any brand, document:

- original in-service date;
- current mileage;
- controlling warranty document;
- date and mileage limit;
- capacity threshold if applicable;
- transfer rules;
- exclusions;
- effect of prior replacement or branded title.

A current manufacturer page is a starting point, not proof of the used car’s exact coverage.

### Step 6 — Audit the listing’s high-value claims

**[VISUAL: claim table with PROVEN / PARTIAL / UNPROVEN.]**

Create a claim ledger.

| Listing claim | Evidence needed | Status |
|---|---|---|
| “Long Range” | vehicle configuration and documentation | proven / partial / unproven |
| “Battery perfect” | records, diagnostic evidence and use-case test | proven / partial / unproven |
| “No accidents” | title/history plus physical inspection | proven / partial / unproven |
| “Full self-driving” or paid software | current vehicle/account entitlement and transfer terms | proven / partial / unproven |
| “Fast charging compatible” | exact inlet, software, adapter and network support | proven / partial / unproven |
| “Warranty remaining” | controlling document, date and mileage | proven / partial / unproven |

Do not accuse the seller of fraud because a claim is unproven. Translate missing evidence into questions, conditions or a lower valuation.

### Step 7 — Generate the inspection and negotiation file

**[VISUAL: one-page buyer file prints.]**

Your output is not merely “good” or “bad.” It is a one-page file:

- verified identity and configuration;
- NHTSA result with date and limitations;
- open questions from service history;
- warranty remaining;
- battery evidence supplied;
- charging compatibility;
- physical inspection requirements;
- insurance quote status;
- out-the-door price;
- conditions required before deposit or purchase.

For example:

> Proceed only if the seller provides the battery-replacement invoice, the manufacturer confirms warranty transfer and an independent inspection finds no collision-related underbody damage.

That is a useful condition. “Seems like a good deal” is not.

### The three result states

**[VERDICT GATE.]**

#### Proceed

- VIN matches all records.
- No unresolved safety gate appears.
- material claims are documented;
- warranty and battery evidence are clear enough;
- charging and total cost fit;
- remaining unknowns are acceptable at the price.

#### Investigate or negotiate

- records are incomplete but obtainable;
- a recall remedy is pending;
- configuration or software claims need proof;
- warranty time is shorter than advertised;
- the price does not yet compensate for uncertainty.

#### Walk

- seller refuses to provide or reconcile the VIN before a deposit;
- VINs or documents remain inconsistent;
- a material title, safety or battery claim is contradicted;
- an active fault or recall risk is unresolved in a way you cannot accept;
- the deal depends on unverifiable high-value features;
- pressure tactics prevent normal due diligence.

### What this audit cannot prove

**[UNKNOWN CARD.]**

A VIN audit cannot prove:

- future reliability;
- complete service history;
- true battery capacity without credible vehicle-specific evidence;
- hidden physical damage;
- insurance cost without a quote;
- that every database is current or complete.

That is why the final step is a qualified physical inspection and a buyer-specific cost and charging review.

### Final verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: THE VIN IS THE START OF THE FILE.]**

The VIN is not a magic truth machine. It is the key that lets you ask better, vehicle-specific questions.

Save the listing. Match the identifier. Run the official safety screen. Reconcile records. Calculate the warranty. Audit the expensive claims. Turn every unknown into a condition.

That process can stop a bad deal before emotion, urgency or a monthly payment takes control.
