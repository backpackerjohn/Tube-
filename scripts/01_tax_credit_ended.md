## Script

**[OPEN — black background. A $4,000 badge appears, then receives a red “ENDED” stamp.]**

**NARRATOR:** If you are shopping for a used electric vehicle in the United States, there is a good chance you have seen a video promising a federal credit worth as much as four thousand dollars.

For a purchase made today, that advice is out of date.

**[ON SCREEN: IRS document card. Highlight “not available for vehicles acquired after Sept. 30, 2025.” Source footer: IRS • verified 2026-07-12.]**

The IRS says the Previously-Owned Clean Vehicle Credit is not available for vehicles acquired after September thirtieth, twenty twenty-five. The old rules could provide thirty percent of a qualifying sale price, up to four thousand dollars, for a qualifying vehicle bought by an eligible buyer from a licensed dealer. But that is historical guidance for acquisitions made by the cutoff. [S01]

So the question is not, “How do I claim the old credit?”

The question is, “How do I evaluate a used EV when the discount I expected has disappeared?”

Today we are going to rebuild the buying strategy around five numbers that still matter: out-the-door price, remaining capability, charging cost, insurance and warranty exposure.

**[TITLE CARD: THE POST-CREDIT USED EV TEST]**

### 1. Reset the comparison price

**[VISUAL: two price tags. Listing price $22,900. Old mental price $18,900 crossed out. Current comparison price $22,900 + local fees.]**

Start by deleting the credit from your mental math. Do not negotiate from a number that no longer exists.

Ask the seller for an itemized, out-the-door quote. Separate the vehicle price from taxes, registration, document fees, optional products, financing and trade-in. Then compare that real number with other used EVs and with a gasoline or hybrid alternative that meets the same job.

The goal is not to prove that an EV is always cheaper. The goal is to compare complete prices for the same use case.

**[ON SCREEN: VERIFIED / OBSERVED / UNKNOWN.]**

Verified: the federal used credit ended for acquisitions after the cutoff. [S01]

Observed: Reuters reported that used-EV prices were moving closer to comparable gasoline vehicles in early twenty twenty-six. It cited a used-EV price premium of one thousand three hundred seventy-six dollars in January, down from two thousand five hundred ninety-one dollars in December. [S07]

Unknown: the actual gap in your city, for your mileage, trim, title history and fees.

### 2. Buy remaining capability, not original sticker price

**[VISUAL: “Original MSRP” slides off screen. Four new cards appear: range fit, charging speed, warranty, condition.]**

A vehicle that once cost sixty thousand dollars is not automatically a bargain because it now costs twenty-five.

Technology can move faster than the depreciation curve. Newer cars may charge faster, travel farther or use a connector that is easier for your routes. Falling prices can create opportunity, but they can also be the market pricing in weaker capability.

The International Energy Agency reported that global average battery-pack prices fell more than twenty-five percent in twenty twenty-four. It also noted that electric cars can have lower lifetime total cost because of lower fuel and maintenance expenses, while purchase-price gaps still vary by market and segment. [S08]

That means your useful question is:

> How much capability remains per dollar for the way I drive?

Score the car on five items:

- enough real-world range with a weather buffer;
- charging that works where you actually travel;
- battery evidence, not just a dashboard guess;
- warranty and recall status;
- local ownership cost.

### 3. Run the charging test before the test drive

**[VISUAL: home, workplace and public-charger icons. Route map with two stations.]**

The Department of Energy tells EV shoppers to begin with daily mileage, home or workplace charging, compatible public stations for longer trips, and the effect of hills, loads, heating and air conditioning. [S02]

Before visiting the car, answer:

One: Where will I charge during a normal week?

Two: What is the price per kilowatt-hour there?

Three: Does this exact car use the connector and charging network available on my common routes?

Four: What happens when my preferred charger is busy or broken?

Five: How much range buffer do winter, heat, hills or towing require?

A discounted vehicle that forces expensive public fast charging may lose part of its operating-cost advantage. A shorter-range vehicle with reliable overnight charging may be the better fit.

### 4. Replace the tax-credit check with a warranty-and-recall check

**[VISUAL: old checklist item “FEDERAL CREDIT” folds away. “WARRANTY CLOCK” and “VIN RECALL” replace it.]**

The old credit once dominated the buying conversation. Now two quieter items deserve more attention.

First, calculate the battery and drive-unit warranty remaining on the exact vehicle. Do not copy the warranty from a current model page onto an older car. Tesla, for example, publishes current battery warranties with a minimum seventy-percent capacity-retention threshold, but it also says the warranty supplied with the specific vehicle is the authoritative document. [S06]

Second, run the VIN through NHTSA’s recall lookup. NHTSA says the tool can identify certain unrepaired recalls, but it may not show already repaired recalls, some newly announced recalls, old recalls or non-safety campaigns. [S04]

So a clean result is one screen in your file, not a complete history.

### 5. Build a local cost worksheet

**[VISUAL: worksheet fills line by line.]**

Use annual numbers, not vague claims.

- Vehicle payment or cash opportunity cost.
- Insurance quote for the exact VIN when possible.
- Registration and local EV fees.
- Home electricity or public-charging rate.
- Miles driven and realistic efficiency.
- Tire condition and replacement timing.
- Charger installation, if needed.
- Warranty products you are considering.
- A repair reserve you choose based on risk tolerance.

Do not let a national “EVs save money” headline replace a local insurance quote and a real charging rate.

### The verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: THE CREDIT IS GONE. THE OPPORTUNITY IS NOT.]**

The end of the federal used-EV credit does not make every used EV a bad buy. Reuters reported that used-EV sales still grew strongly in twenty twenty-five and January twenty twenty-six as prices fell. [S07]

But it changes the discipline.

**Buy candidate** when the out-the-door price is competitive without imaginary incentives, the car passes the battery and VIN checks, and charging works at your real rates.

**Wait or negotiate** when the price still assumes an expired benefit, the warranty evidence is vague or the charging plan depends on one unreliable station.

**Walk** when the seller refuses the VIN or itemized price, when the car cannot serve your routes with a safe buffer, or when the deal only works after counting a credit you cannot receive.

**[CHECKLIST ON SCREEN]**

Before you contact the seller:

1. Remove the old federal credit from your math.
2. Get the out-the-door price.
3. Run the charging-fit test.
4. Calculate warranty remaining.
5. Check the VIN and document the limits.
6. Get an insurance quote.
7. Decide from total cost and remaining capability.
