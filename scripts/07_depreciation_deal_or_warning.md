# Script 07 — EV Depreciation: Bargain, Warning or Both?

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Money File  
**Thumbnail:** `PRICE DROP: WHY`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — original price falls dramatically to a used listing price. A green “BARGAIN” and amber “WARNING” appear at the same time.]**

**NARRATOR:** A used electric vehicle has lost half its original price.

That can be the reason to buy it—and the reason to slow down.

Depreciation transfers value from the first owner to the second buyer. But a low price can also reflect technology change, weak charging capability, warranty expiration, expensive insurance or uncertainty about resale.

Today we will separate a useful discount from a capability discount.

**[TITLE: PRICE DROP = OPPORTUNITY + INFORMATION]**

### What the market is doing

Reuters reported that U.S. used-EV sales totaled three hundred seventy-eight thousand one hundred forty vehicles in twenty twenty-five, roughly thirty-five percent above the prior year. January twenty twenty-six sales were about twenty-one percent higher than a year earlier. It also reported that the used-EV premium over comparable gasoline vehicles narrowed to one thousand three hundred seventy-six dollars in January from two thousand five hundred ninety-one dollars in December. [S07]

The same report described rapid improvements in battery technology, range and software as factors pushing older EV prices down.

The International Energy Agency reported that global average battery-pack prices fell more than twenty-five percent in twenty twenty-four. [S08]

So falling used prices are not automatically evidence of failure. They are partly the result of lower technology costs, new competition and newer capability.

### The four reasons a price falls

**[VISUAL: four quadrants.]**

#### 1. Normal age and mileage

Every vehicle loses value as it ages, accumulates miles and uses warranty coverage.

#### 2. New-car price changes

When a manufacturer cuts new-car prices or introduces incentives, the used market must adjust. A buyer should compare the used car with today’s new alternative, not the old window sticker.

#### 3. Capability gap

Newer vehicles may offer more range, faster charging, improved efficiency, different connectors, better software or more useful warranty. The older car can still be excellent if you do not need the newer capability.

#### 4. Model-specific risk or weak demand

A recall, poor charging experience, expensive repair reputation, insurance issue, discontinued support or unusual configuration may reduce demand. This category requires evidence, not rumor.

### The capability-per-dollar test

**[VISUAL: price divided into five capability blocks.]**

Compare two vehicles with a five-column matrix:

1. conservative usable range for your route;
2. charging time and network fit;
3. warranty protection remaining;
4. safety/recall and documented condition;
5. annual local ownership cost.

Then divide the out-the-door price by the useful years or miles you reasonably expect for your use case. This is a scenario, not a resale forecast.

The cheapest sticker price loses when it requires more charging time than you accept, cannot cover a route, lacks evidence or carries a local insurance premium that erases the discount.

### The “new alternative” check

**[VISUAL: used car on left, current new/used alternatives on right.]**

A used EV should be compared against:

- a newer used version;
- a new EV after current, actually available incentives;
- a comparable hybrid or gasoline vehicle;
- the cost of keeping your existing car.

Do not use the expired federal used-EV credit in a current purchase comparison. The IRS says it is unavailable for acquisitions after September thirtieth, twenty twenty-five. [S01]

Request out-the-door prices. Compare financing terms as well as cash price. A longer loan on a rapidly depreciating asset can increase the period in which the balance exceeds the car’s market value.

### The battery-risk check

**[VISUAL: price discount versus evidence discount.]**

A steep discount should buy either capability or compensate for uncertainty.

Recurrent’s data suggests battery replacement outside major recalls is uncommon in its community, especially for newer generations, but it also states that the data is not representative of every EV. [S09]

So do not add an imaginary full-pack replacement to every deal. Do not ignore the battery either.

Instead ask:

- exact pack and model year;
- recall/replacement history;
- credible range/diagnostic evidence;
- warranty remaining and threshold;
- whether the remaining capability fits your use.

If the seller cannot supply evidence, the price should reflect the uncertainty—or you should walk.

### The resale humility rule

**[VISUAL: future price chart fades into a question mark.]**

No one can promise what your used EV will be worth in three years.

Future value depends on fuel and electricity prices, new models, interest rates, policy, battery costs, brand decisions and supply. Do not buy a depreciated EV as a guaranteed investment.

Use three exit scenarios:

- **Keep:** you drive it until resale value matters less.
- **Normal sale:** assume continued depreciation without pretending to know the exact number.
- **Stress sale:** ask whether you could tolerate a lower-than-expected value after a job, housing or charging change.

A buyer with a short ownership horizon should be more sensitive to future resale than a buyer planning to keep the car for many years.

### Bargain, warning or both?

**[DECISION MATRIX.]**

A falling price is a **bargain signal** when:

- the car still performs the job;
- charging fits;
- warranty and condition are documented;
- the discount is larger than the capability you give up;
- insurance and local costs remain acceptable.

It is a **warning signal** when:

- the discount follows a capability you actually need;
- warranty expiration is near;
- seller evidence is weak;
- the model has an unresolved safety or service issue;
- public-only charging or insurance erases the savings;
- the deal depends on a resale recovery.

It is often **both**. A rational buyer uses the warning to negotiate the bargain.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: BUY THE JOB, NOT THE OLD MSRP.]**

Do not ask, “How much did this car lose?”

Ask:

> “At today’s out-the-door price, how much useful capability, protection and charging fit am I buying?”

**Buy candidate:** the car passes that test and the discount compensates for older technology.

**Wait or negotiate:** you need stronger evidence or a larger discount.

**Walk:** the price is low because the vehicle cannot serve your use case, or the seller wants you to anchor to an irrelevant original MSRP.
