# Script 04 — Why Full-Charge Range Does Not Prove Battery Health

**Target length:** 8–10 minutes  
**Series:** Battery Truth  
**Thumbnail:** `RANGE LIE?`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — dashboard reads 100% and 280 miles. Freeze frame.]**

**NARRATOR:** One hundred percent. Two hundred eighty miles.

It looks like a battery-health test. It is not.

That dashboard number can be useful, but it is an estimate produced inside a system affected by software, efficiency assumptions, temperature, recent driving and battery reserves. Recurrent explicitly warns that range availability is not the same thing as battery degradation. [S09]

Today we will explain what the number can tell you, what it cannot tell you, and the four-piece evidence test to use instead.

**[TITLE: RANGE ESTIMATE ≠ STATE OF HEALTH]**

### What the dashboard is trying to do

**[VISUAL: battery energy → vehicle efficiency → predicted miles.]**

A range estimate converts available energy into expected distance. To do that, the car needs assumptions about how efficiently it will use energy.

Change the assumptions and the miles can change even if the physical battery does not suddenly gain or lose the same percentage of capacity.

Recent high-speed driving, winter heating, steep terrain, wheel and tire choices, software logic and climate use can all change the distance the car expects to travel.

The Department of Energy tells buyers to account for hills, heavy loads, heating and air conditioning because these can reduce range. [S02]

So two identical batteries can display different expected miles under different conditions.

### Why a stable number can still hide aging

**[VISUAL: software efficiency improvement and reserved energy offset gradual capacity loss.]**

Recurrent notes another complication: some automakers can maintain the driver’s apparent range through efficiency changes or by releasing reserved battery capacity over time. Its research says several vehicles can appear to retain original range while the battery has still physically aged. [S09]

This does not mean the display is dishonest. It means the display answers a different question:

> “How far does the system expect the car to travel?”

It does not directly answer:

> “What percentage of the battery’s original usable capacity remains under a standardized test?”

### The four-piece test

**[VISUAL: four quadrants.]**

#### Piece 1 — Same-state comparison

Capture the displayed state of charge and estimated miles together. Compare like with like: same charge percentage, similar temperature and similar recent use where possible.

One screenshot without context is weak evidence. A series over time is more useful.

#### Piece 2 — Energy consumption

Look for recent efficiency data in kilowatt-hours per distance, where the vehicle provides it. A car driven inefficiently can show fewer predicted miles with a healthy battery. A carefully driven car can show an optimistic estimate.

Do not compare displayed miles without comparing energy use.

#### Piece 3 — Controlled route or credible diagnostic

A safe, permission-based route test can compare energy consumed with distance traveled under recorded conditions. A manufacturer-supported battery test or qualified diagnostic report may add stronger evidence, depending on the model.

Read the method. A large “92%” badge with no explanation of data source, calibration or uncertainty is not automatically reliable.

#### Piece 4 — Warranty and use-case fit

Battery warranty language often uses a capacity threshold. Tesla’s current warranty page, for example, lists minimum seventy-percent retention for Model 3 and Model Y battery coverage variants, while warning that the vehicle-specific warranty is authoritative. [S06]

A car above a warranty threshold can still have less range than when new. More important, the remaining range may or may not fit your driving.

Use the conservative evidence to answer:

- Can it complete my normal day with a buffer?
- Can I recharge reliably?
- What happens in my worst common weather?

### Three examples

**[VISUAL: illustrative scenarios labeled EXAMPLE, not real vehicles.]**

**Example A:** The display is low, but recent efficiency is poor after fast winter highway driving. A controlled test and warmer conditions improve the estimate. The battery may not be the main cause.

**Example B:** The display looks close to the original rating, but software and reserve management are supporting the user experience. The exact state of health remains unknown without stronger evidence.

**Example C:** The display and a controlled route both show materially reduced capability, but the car still covers a forty-mile commute with dependable overnight charging. It may be a good fit at the right price even if it would be a poor road-trip choice.

The examples illustrate reasoning; they are not prevalence claims.

### The seller conversation

**[VISUAL: buyer question list.]**

Instead of asking, “What range does it show at full charge?” ask:

1. Can you show the charge percentage and range on the same screen?
2. What temperature and recent driving produced that estimate?
3. Can you show recent energy consumption?
4. Has the battery or a module been replaced?
5. Is a manufacturer battery-health test available?
6. What battery warranty remains for this VIN?
7. May a qualified EV technician inspect it?

A cooperative seller does not guarantee a good battery, but refusal to provide basic evidence increases uncertainty.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: DASHBOARD = CLUE.]**

Use displayed range as one clue in a stack.

**Buy candidate:** the display, efficiency history, diagnostic or controlled-route evidence and warranty all tell a consistent story that fits your use case.

**Wait or negotiate:** the displayed number is the seller’s only evidence, context is missing or the range fits only under optimistic assumptions.

**Walk:** an active fault, unresolved safety issue or conservative capability failure remains.

The correct sentence is not, “The dashboard proves the battery is healthy.”

It is:

> “The dashboard estimate is consistent—or inconsistent—with the rest of the evidence.”

**[END SCREEN: WILL IT CHARGE?]**

A healthy battery can still be the wrong purchase when the car cannot charge where you need it. Next, we run the charging compatibility check.
