# Script 09 — The Real Used-EV Cost Worksheet

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Money File  
**Thumbnail:** `PRICE ISN’T COST`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Script

**[OPEN — listing price $19,900. Additional rows slide in until the total decision fills the screen.]**

**NARRATOR:** The listing price is the easiest number in a used-EV purchase—and one of the least useful by itself.

A cheaper car can cost more when insurance, public charging, tires, installation or depreciation are added. A more expensive car can be the better value when it saves time, has stronger warranty protection and fits your route.

Today we are building a twelve-line worksheet that turns “EVs are cheaper” or “EVs are expensive” into a local scenario you can actually compare.

**[TITLE: THE USED EV TOTAL-COST WORKSHEET]**

### Rule one — compare the same job

**[VISUAL: commute, passenger count, cargo, road trip, climate.]**

Choose alternatives that perform the same job:

- same passenger and cargo need;
- same routine mileage;
- same long-trip expectation;
- same climate and terrain;
- similar safety and comfort requirements.

Do not compare a small used EV with a large gasoline SUV unless both truly meet the use case.

The Department of Energy advises buyers to evaluate daily driving, longer routes, charging and range reductions from climate, hills or loads. [S02]

### Line 1 — Out-the-door purchase price

Get the written price including dealer fees and required charges, then separate taxes, registration, optional warranties and financing.

Do not subtract the expired federal used clean vehicle credit from a current purchase. The IRS says it is unavailable for vehicles acquired after September thirtieth, twenty twenty-five. [S01]

Check current state, local and utility programs separately and verify eligibility from official sources.

### Line 2 — Financing or cash cost

**[VISUAL: principal, rate, term, total interest.]**

For financing, compare total paid—not monthly payment alone.

Record:

- down payment;
- amount financed;
- interest rate;
- term;
- total interest;
- prepayment terms.

A low monthly payment created by a long term can keep you owing more than the vehicle’s market value for longer. Do not predict resale; run a stress scenario.

For cash, decide whether you want to include an opportunity-cost assumption. Label it clearly as an assumption, not a fee.

### Line 3 — Insurance

Get a quote for the exact model and preferably the exact VIN before purchase.

Use the same driver, coverage, deductible and address for every vehicle compared. A generic national average is not your premium.

Record annual cost and the collision/comprehensive deductible you would need to absorb after a loss.

### Line 4 — Registration, taxes and local EV fees

State and local rules vary. Enter actual annual registration, personal-property tax where applicable, inspection and EV-specific fees from official local sources.

Do not borrow a number from another state.

### Line 5 — Home-charging setup

**[VISUAL: no installation / existing outlet / licensed installation quote.]**

Enter zero only when you have verified that no equipment or electrical work is needed.

Possible inputs:

- charging cable or wall unit;
- licensed electrician quote;
- permit;
- utility upgrade;
- landlord or HOA costs;
- repair/restoration after installation.

This channel does not instruct electrical installation. Get a qualified local quote.

### Line 6 — Electricity

Use your real rate structure and expected charging mix.

Calculation:

`annual miles ÷ realistic miles per kWh × price per kWh`

Then adjust for charging losses if your source/calculator includes them, and label the assumption.

Create three cases:

- home/work dominant;
- mixed;
- public-only.

The IEA says EVs often benefit from lower fuel and maintenance expenses over their lifetime, but affordability varies. [S08] Your rate and charging behavior decide the local result.

### Line 7 — Public-charging extras

Add membership fees, session fees, parking, idle fees and detour cost. If charging requires a dedicated weekly trip, assign a time value only if you choose to—and label it as personal.

### Line 8 — Tires

**[VISUAL: tread, replacement timing, quote.]**

Inspect tread, age, damage and matching set. Get a current quote for the correct size and load/speed specification. Enter the likely timing within your ownership horizon.

Do not claim all EVs “eat tires.” Driving style, weight, alignment, tire choice and torque can affect wear. Price the actual vehicle’s tires.

### Line 9 — Routine maintenance and wear

EVs avoid some gasoline-engine service, but they still require tires, brakes, suspension, cabin filters, wipers, fluids specified by the manufacturer and ordinary repairs.

FuelEconomy.gov notes that electric motors require less maintenance than internal-combustion engines, while battery replacement can be costly if failure occurs. [S03]

Use the manufacturer schedule and inspection, not a slogan such as “maintenance-free.”

### Line 10 — Warranty and repair exposure

Record:

- basic warranty remaining;
- battery/drive-unit coverage;
- capacity threshold;
- exclusions;
- service-contract cost if considered;
- emergency reserve chosen by you.

Do not enter the full cost of a new battery as an inevitable annual expense. Recurrent’s community data found replacement outside major recalls was uncommon, but the data is not representative of every EV. [S09]

Instead, display warranty protection and uncertainty separately.

### Line 11 — Depreciation scenario

**[VISUAL: keep, normal sale, stress sale.]**

No one can guarantee future value. Run three exits:

- keep long enough that resale matters less;
- normal sale using a conservative assumption you label;
- stress sale with a materially lower price.

The worksheet is useful even when you leave depreciation as a range rather than a precise number.

### Line 12 — Time and fit penalties

Add non-cash notes:

- charging detours;
- road-trip stop tolerance;
- rental need for rare trips;
- inability to tow or carry required loads;
- time saved by home charging;
- access to a second household vehicle.

Do not convert everything to dollars unless the buyer chooses a value. Some constraints are gates, not costs.

### The comparison output

**[VISUAL: EV A, hybrid B, keep current car C.]**

Show:

- year-one cash cost;
- annual operating cost;
- three-year scenario range;
- warranty/repair exposure;
- charging time and fit;
- unresolved unknowns.

A single “cost per month” hides too much.

### Verdict

**[VERDICT CARD: PRICE IS ONE LINE.]**

A used EV is a **buy candidate** when its complete scenario is competitive and its non-cash fit works.

It is a **wait or negotiate** when insurance, installation, tires or missing warranty evidence erode the listing discount.

It is a **walk for this use case** when public-only charging, route failure or financing risk makes the complete decision unacceptable.

The worksheet does not exist to make the EV win. It exists to make the honest option win.

**[END SCREEN: CHECK THIS VIN.]**

There is one final pre-purchase screen before the listing becomes a real deal: the VIN and recall audit.
