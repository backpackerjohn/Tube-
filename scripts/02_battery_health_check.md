# Script 02 — How to Check a Used EV Battery Before You Buy

**Target length:** 9–11 minutes  
**Series:** Battery Truth  
**Thumbnail:** `BATTERY CLUE`  
**Last source verification for this draft:** 2026-07-12

## Production note

This is a buyer-screening workflow, not a high-voltage inspection tutorial. Do not show opening, probing or repairing a traction battery.

## Script

**[OPEN — vehicle listing on screen. Seller text: “Battery perfect. Full charge shows 276 miles.” A stamp appears: CLUE, NOT PROOF.]**

**NARRATOR:** A seller says the battery is perfect because the dashboard shows two hundred seventy-six miles at one hundred percent.

That number may be useful. It is not a battery health certificate.

A used EV battery can be the most expensive and least visible part of the deal. The right response is not panic, and it is not blind trust. It is an evidence stack.

Today, we are building that stack from six checks you can perform before you pay—and one honest category called “still unknown.”

**[TITLE: THE USED EV BATTERY EVIDENCE STACK]**

### Check 1 — Identify the exact battery, not just the model name

**[VISUAL: model name splits into year, trim, battery size, chemistry, drivetrain.]**

Start with identity.

Record the model year, trim, drivetrain, original usable battery size if published, and any battery variants sold under the same badge. A listing that says only “long range” may not be enough. VIN decoding, build information, manufacturer documentation and the vehicle’s own screens can help, but do not guess when a model changed battery chemistry or capacity during a production run.

Why does this matter? Because original range, charging behavior, warranty and comparison baselines can differ. You cannot calculate degradation against the wrong starting point.

**[ON SCREEN: UNKNOWN UNTIL VERIFIED — exact pack variant.]**

### Check 2 — Separate failure risk from normal aging

**[VISUAL: two paths. Defect/failure and gradual capacity loss.]**

People often compress every battery question into one fear: replacement.

But two different issues exist.

One is failure or a manufacturing defect. The other is gradual capacity loss that reduces usable range.

Recurrent’s twenty twenty-five research looked at a community of more than thirty thousand EVs. Outside major recalls, it reported battery replacements in under four percent across all model years. It also reported higher replacement rates in first-generation EVs and much lower rates for newer vehicles. The company explicitly warns that its community is not representative of every EV and that data access varies by make and model. [S09]

That evidence argues against assuming every old battery is about to fail. It does not tell you that this specific car is healthy.

Your job is to ask both questions:

- Is there a known defect, recall or replacement history?
- How much useful capability remains for my driving?

### Check 3 — Run the recall and replacement-history screen

**[VISUAL: NHTSA recall page workflow, then service-invoice folders.]**

Enter the VIN in NHTSA’s recall lookup. NHTSA says the search can show certain unrepaired safety recalls. It also states the limits: repaired recalls may not appear, some new recalls may not yet include every VIN, old recalls and non-safety campaigns may be absent. [S04]

Then ask for service records that answer:

- Was the high-voltage battery or a module replaced?
- Was replacement performed under recall or warranty?
- Is there documentation with date and mileage?
- Did the replacement reset or extend any warranty? Do not assume it did.
- Are software campaigns complete?

A replacement is not automatically bad news. A documented replacement under a manufacturer program may mean newer hardware. But the date, part, warranty and reason matter.

**[THREE TRUTHS BOX]**

Verified: the recall status you can see today.

Observed: invoices and vehicle history supplied.

Unknown: repairs or campaigns not in the sources you checked.

### Check 4 — Collect more than one range clue

**[VISUAL: dashboard estimate, trip efficiency, charge history and diagnostic report orbit a center label “EVIDENCE.”]**

The full-charge dashboard estimate is one clue. Recurrent specifically notes that range availability is not the same as battery degradation. Software, efficiency and reserved capacity can affect the experience and the displayed result. [S09]

Build a better picture:

1. Photograph the state of charge and estimated range at the same moment.
2. Record recent energy efficiency if the car exposes it.
3. Ask how the estimate was produced: warm weather, winter, highway or city use?
4. Compare a controlled route before and after, noting temperature, speed, elevation and climate use.
5. Request a manufacturer-supported battery-health test or a reputable diagnostic report when available.
6. Read the test’s method and uncertainty. A percentage without methodology is decoration.

Do not perform a highway range test on a vehicle you do not own without permission, insurance and a safe plan. A pre-purchase inspection by a qualified EV technician can be worth more than an improvised test.

### Check 5 — Read the warranty as a threshold, not a promise of new-car range

**[VISUAL: warranty clock and capacity gauge stop at 70%.]**

Many battery warranties use years, mileage and a capacity threshold.

Tesla’s current warranty page, for example, lists Model 3 and Model Y variants with eight-year mileage limits and a minimum seventy-percent capacity-retention threshold. Tesla also says the warranty supplied with the specific vehicle is authoritative. [S06]

That means three things.

First, current web terms may not match an older model year.

Second, seventy percent capacity does not mean the battery is dead. It means the manufacturer’s stated capacity threshold for that warranty example.

Third, a car can have noticeable range loss and still be above the warranty threshold.

For the exact VIN, record:

- in-service date;
- current mileage;
- battery/drive-unit warranty end date and mileage;
- capacity threshold;
- transfer rules;
- exclusions;
- process required to document a claim.

### Check 6 — Convert battery evidence into use-case range

**[VISUAL: daily route 42 miles; winter buffer; charging access; result PASS.]**

Battery health is not only a percentage. It is whether the remaining capability serves the buyer.

The Department of Energy recommends evaluating daily mileage, charging options, compatible stations on longer routes, and reduced range from hills, loads, heating and air conditioning. [S02]

Use a conservative scenario.

Suppose your longest routine day is eighty miles. Add a weather and detour buffer that reflects your location. Then decide how much charge you are comfortable using between stops. Compare that requirement with the evidence-supported range of the vehicle—not the most flattering number in the listing.

A lower-capacity car with dependable home charging can be a better fit than a longer-range car that depends on expensive or unreliable public charging.

### The unknown category

**[VISUAL: dark card labeled UNKNOWN UNTIL THE EXACT CAR IS TESTED.]**

After all six checks, some things may remain unknown:

- true usable capacity under a standardized test;
- cell imbalance or intermittent faults not exposed in a simple report;
- future failure;
- how the car was stored for every day of its life;
- whether a diagnostic service’s algorithm is accurate for this model.

Do not hide those gaps. Price and risk tolerance must absorb them.

### The EV Second Opinion battery verdict

**[SCORECARD BUILDS.]**

Award battery points only for evidence:

- exact pack identified;
- recall and replacement history checked;
- multiple range/efficiency clues agree;
- credible diagnostic result where available;
- warranty clock and threshold documented;
- remaining capability fits the buyer’s routes.

**Buy candidate:** the evidence is consistent, the range fits with a buffer, and the remaining warranty or price compensates for uncertainty.

**Wait or negotiate:** the car may fit, but the seller will not provide records, the diagnostic evidence conflicts, or the price assumes stronger battery health than you can verify.

**Walk:** there is an unresolved safety recall, active battery fault, material misrepresentation, or the conservative range cannot serve your use case.

**[CHECKLIST]**

Before paying:

1. Identify exact pack.
2. Run VIN recall check.
3. Obtain battery/replacement records.
4. Gather dashboard, efficiency and diagnostic clues.
5. Extract exact warranty terms.
6. Test against your real routes.
7. Price the remaining unknowns.
