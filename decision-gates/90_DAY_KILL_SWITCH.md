# 90-Day Kill / Iterate / Scale Framework

## Why this exists

Creators often keep a weak thesis alive by moving the goalposts after every upload. This document commits the channel to a decision **before** launch.

The thresholds below are planning hypotheses for this specific channel. They are not claimed as universal YouTube benchmarks.

## Test design

Minimum test:

- 10 original long-form videos;
- approximately one upload per week;
- at least four buyer/model files;
- at least three evergreen problem videos;
- at least one current policy/market video;
- no purchased views or unrelated audience promotion;
- consistent brand, voice, source treatment and end-screen architecture;
- a three-video buffer at launch.

Do not judge the thesis after one upload. Do not extend the test beyond 13 weeks merely because the answer is uncomfortable.

## Primary question

> Is there evidence that a specific audience will repeatedly choose evidence-first used-EV buying decisions from a faceless channel?

Views alone are insufficient. The test combines discovery, satisfaction, conversion and repeat behavior.

## Metrics recorded per video

At 24 hours, 7 days and 30 days:

- impressions;
- click-through rate by traffic source;
- first-30-second retention;
- average percentage viewed;
- watch time per impression;
- subscribers gained per 1,000 views;
- returning viewers;
- end-screen click rate;
- comments with active purchase intent;
- corrections or trust objections;
- production hours and direct cost.

Use the final five videos as the clearest measure of an improved system, while retaining the full ten-video record.

## Green: scale the thesis

Scale when the test shows **all three evidence classes** below.

### A. Discovery evidence — at least one

- two of the final five videos each receive at least 10,000 organic impressions within 30 days; or
- one video reaches at least 5,000 organic views within 30 days and a second reaches at least 2,000; or
- search/browse impressions rise across three consecutive comparable uploads even if absolute numbers remain lower.

### B. Viewer-satisfaction evidence — at least two

- median first-30-second retention of final five videos is at least 65%;
- median average percentage viewed is at least 38% for 8–13 minute videos;
- median watch time per impression improves at least 20% from the first three to the final three;
- comments repeatedly cite the evidence method, checklist or a changed purchase action.

### C. Channel-conversion evidence — at least two

- median subscriber conversion is at least 8 subscribers per 1,000 views on the final five;
- returning viewers increase in each of the final three 28-day comparison windows;
- at least 25 independent comments across the test contain a specific model, deadline, VIN, charging situation or buying action;
- end-screen clicks to the next decision average at least 1.5% of eligible viewers.

### Green action

- Maintain or increase model-file cadence.
- Produce the two strongest follow-up topics within four weeks.
- Begin one carefully screened affiliate test.
- Build the free Used EV Evidence Sheet.
- Do not increase output until accuracy and source-refresh capacity are stable.

## Yellow: evidence exists, but the product needs correction

Yellow applies when discovery occurs but one of satisfaction or conversion is weak, or when audience behavior concentrates around a narrower subtopic.

Typical patterns and actions:

### Pattern 1 — High impressions, low CTR

Likely problem: packaging or unclear value proposition.

Action:

- test decision-led thumbnails against evidence-object thumbnails;
- shorten titles and put the model/problem first;
- remove generic terms such as “complete guide”;
- retain the thesis for four additional uploads only if watch metrics are healthy after click.

### Pattern 2 — Good CTR, weak first 30 seconds

Likely problem: the hook overpromises or method explanation is slow.

Action:

- show the verdict condition in the first 20 seconds;
- move methodology detail after the first decisive fact;
- cut channel-identification animation below three seconds;
- compare title/thumbnail promise with opening sentence.

### Pattern 3 — Good retention, low subscriber conversion

Likely problem: useful one-off answers without a recurring promise.

Action:

- make the next decision explicit;
- build model and problem playlists;
- repeat the Verified / Observed / Unknown product more visibly;
- prioritize serial buyer files over isolated tax or news updates.

### Pattern 4 — Tesla wins; cross-brand videos stall

Likely problem: the audience is Tesla-led rather than category-led.

Action:

- publish two Tesla files and one adjacent affordable EV file;
- track returning viewers crossing between them;
- consider a Tesla-heavy season, not an immediate permanent rebrand;
- retain universal battery, charging and cost videos.

### Pattern 5 — Search wins; browse stalls

Likely problem: topics solve narrow queries but lack emotional stakes.

Action:

- retain evergreen search library;
- package model files around a costly choice;
- lead with price, range or warranty consequence rather than terminology;
- add listing autopsies without identifying private sellers.

### Pattern 6 — Audience wants physical inspection footage

Likely problem: document-led trust is insufficient.

Action:

- partner later with qualified inspectors for remote/audio expertise, subject to the no-contact guardrail ending after this package;
- commission licensed close-up demonstrations;
- do not fake ownership or use copied repair footage;
- if evidence-led videos remain weak, pivot rather than violate the format.

### Yellow extension limit

Allow **four additional videos over no more than six weeks** with one documented hypothesis. Do not make multiple major changes at once. If no primary metric improves, move to red.

## Red: kill or pivot

Trigger red when, after ten completed videos and normal optimization, most of the following are true:

- fewer than two videos exceed 1,000 organic views within 30 days;
- median CTR on final five is below 3% after adequate impressions;
- median average percentage viewed on final five is below 28%;
- median subscriber conversion is below 3 per 1,000 views;
- no upward returning-viewer trend appears;
- fewer than 10 high-intent buyer comments appear across the test;
- source-heavy production exceeds a sustainable workload with no sign of audience pull;
- the most viewed content is unrelated to the central used-EV due-diligence promise;
- repeated trust objections show the faceless format is the barrier rather than execution.

A single missed threshold does not kill the thesis. A pattern across discovery, satisfaction and conversion does.

## Red actions

### Pivot A — Existing-owner EV decisions

Reuse the strongest research assets for:

- keep versus sell;
- warranty claim preparation;
- charging-cost optimization;
- repair-versus-trade frameworks;
- range-change diagnosis without unsafe repair advice.

This keeps the brand and expertise while targeting a recurring owner audience.

### Pivot B — Tesla used-buyer specialization

Use only when Tesla content produces materially higher repeat viewing and conversion, not merely one viral result.

### Pivot C — Home Wi-Fi diagnosis

This was the independent runner-up in the candidate tournament. It has strong demand and asset-based potential, but requires a new brand and a fresh 10-video test. Do not mix it into EV Second Opinion.

### Kill

Archive the experiment when:

- the red pattern remains after one controlled yellow extension;
- producing accurate episodes is economically or personally unsustainable;
- the channel requires copied footage, paid verdicts or unsafe claims to perform.

Do not delete the public evidence library solely because the thesis failed. Preserve useful videos and add update notes where needed.

## Topic-level kill switch

A recurring series is paused after three comparable episodes when:

- it receives less than half the channel median watch time per impression;
- subscriber conversion is less than half the channel median;
- the same audience does not move to the next playlist video;
- production cost is more than twice the channel median without strategic benefit.

A series can survive low views when it supports trust, search entry or a high-performing playlist, but that reason must be written.

## Integrity override

Stop or delay an episode regardless of expected performance when:

- the controlling warranty or policy cannot be verified;
- a safety claim depends on anecdote;
- the individual VIN or configuration is uncertain;
- a sponsor requires editorial approval or positive treatment;
- visual rights are unclear;
- the conclusion is stronger than the evidence.
