# Channel Blueprint — EV Second Opinion

## One-line thesis

**EV Second Opinion is the faceless, evidence-first buying desk for used electric vehicles.** It checks the battery, charging fit, warranty, recalls and real ownership cost before giving a conditional **Buy / Wait / Walk** verdict.

## Channel name

# EV Second Opinion

Alternates held in reserve:

- Used EV Evidence
- The EV Buyer File
- Battery Before Buy
- EV Due Diligence

**Why the selected name wins:** It sounds independent, not anti-EV or pro-EV. It promises an additional check before a high-stakes purchase and can expand into ownership questions without changing the brand.

## Tagline

> **Know the battery. Know the cost. Buy the right used EV.**

## Audience promise

Every episode answers one buyer question using a visible evidence chain. The viewer leaves with:

1. the facts that matter;
2. what remains uncertain;
3. a checklist they can use on an actual listing;
4. a verdict that states the conditions under which the car is a buy, wait or walk.

## Primary audience

**U.S. used-EV shoppers, roughly 28–55, considering a $12,000–$35,000 vehicle.** They are comfortable researching online but do not want to become battery engineers. Typical situations:

- replacing a gasoline commuter with a used Model 3, Bolt, Leaf, Ioniq 5, Mach-E or similar vehicle;
- seeing a steeply depreciated premium EV and wondering whether it is a bargain;
- buying for a teenager or young adult;
- living in an apartment or renting and unsure about charging;
- comparing an EV with a hybrid at the same price;
- worried that one battery failure could erase the savings.

The price band is a strategy assumption, not a market statistic.

## Secondary audiences

- current EV owners deciding whether to keep, repair or sell;
- family members helping a buyer;
- independent dealers who want a transparent checklist;
- EV-curious viewers learning how ownership differs from gasoline.

## The enemy

The channel is against **uninformed confidence**, not against a brand or powertrain.

Examples:

- “The dashboard says 280 miles, so the battery is healthy.”
- “It has no open recall, so the service history is clean.”
- “EVs need no maintenance.”
- “Battery replacement is inevitable.”
- “A huge depreciation drop automatically means bargain.”
- “The old tax-credit video I watched still applies.”

## Positioning statement

For used-EV shoppers who are overwhelmed by conflicting owner stories, dealership claims and outdated incentives, **EV Second Opinion** is the YouTube buyer-intelligence channel that turns official records, battery evidence, warranty terms, charging reality and total-cost math into a clear decision. Unlike broad automotive review channels, it does not need a press car, host personality or road-test spectacle; the product is a transparent due-diligence system.

## Why the channel should work

### 1. Demand is already proven

Five recent used-EV buyer videos in the API sample attracted 257K–469K views across several vehicles and creators. [Y01–Y05] vidIQ also surfaced a cluster of lower-competition buyer and battery queries. [V01]

### 2. The category is growing and changing

Reuters reported strong U.S. used-EV sales growth in 2025 and January 2026. [S07] The federal used clean vehicle credit ended for acquisitions after September 30, 2025, making older advice stale. [S01]

### 3. The format fits the guardrails

The videos use:

- original scripts and narration;
- custom charts, matrices and calculators;
- public official documents;
- annotated screenshots captured for the episode;
- maps and route scenarios;
- licensed stock only as visual punctuation;
- simple original motion graphics and diagrams.

No host, vehicle, camera or custom-shot road footage is required.

### 4. The audience has commercial intent

A viewer deciding on a vehicle is also deciding on charging, insurance, financing, history reports, tires and accessories. Commercial relevance is credible even before sponsorship, but no earnings outcome is promised.

### 5. The channel can compound

Every model-year buyer file becomes a node in a library. Broad problem videos feed model videos; model videos feed warranty, charging and cost playlists. Older videos are refreshed with pinned corrections and annual editions.

## The EV Second Opinion Score

Each buyer-file episode uses a 100-point score. It is an editorial framework, not a scientific certification.

| Category | Points | Required evidence |
|---|---:|---|
| Battery evidence | 25 | available state-of-health evidence, recall history, thermal management, age/mileage context |
| Charging fit | 20 | connector, peak/typical charging behavior, route and home/work access |
| Warranty protection | 15 | remaining term, capacity threshold, transferability, exclusions |
| Safety/recall status | 15 | NHTSA model/VIN check plus limitations [S04] |
| Ownership-cost resilience | 15 | insurance quote, tires, registration, charging and likely wear items using viewer-local inputs |
| Price/value evidence | 10 | dated comparable listings with trim, mileage, geography and fees |

Verdict bands:

- **80–100: Buy candidate** — proceed only after the vehicle-specific checks.
- **65–79: Conditional buy** — price or use case must compensate for a known weakness.
- **50–64: Wait / negotiate** — evidence is incomplete or value is not strong enough.
- **Below 50: Walk for this use case** — not a universal condemnation of the model.

A score cannot override a failed safety, title or charging-fit gate.

## Mandatory “three truths” box

Every episode displays:

- **Verified:** what an official or primary source establishes.
- **Observed:** what the dated market/API sample shows.
- **Unknown:** what cannot be proven without the individual VIN, diagnostic result, insurance quote or physical inspection.

This box is the core trust mechanism.

## Content pillars

### Pillar 1 — Used EV Buyer Files

Model-year-specific episodes using the full score. Examples:

- Used Tesla Model 3: the seven checks
- Used Chevy Bolt after the battery recall
- Used Ioniq 5: charging and recall file
- Used Nissan Leaf: when the low price fits
- Used Mach-E: warranty, charging and depreciation

### Pillar 2 — Battery Truth

- How to evaluate battery health
- Why dashboard range is not state of health
- Degradation versus failure
- Capacity warranty thresholds
- Heat, cold and charging behavior
- What a replacement record can mean

### Pillar 3 — Charging Reality

- apartment ownership test
- Level 1 versus Level 2 math
- route compatibility
- connector transitions
- cold-weather buffer
- public-fast-charging cost scenarios

### Pillar 4 — Money File

- total-cost worksheet
- depreciation as opportunity versus warning
- insurance quote protocol
- tire and registration inputs
- financing and warranty product questions
- current incentive/policy updates

### Pillar 5 — Listing Autopsy

A real or anonymized listing is reconstructed on screen. The episode verifies every seller claim it can, marks what cannot be verified and produces a buyer question list. No accusation is made without evidence.

### Pillar 6 — Ownership Decision

- keep versus sell
- repair versus trade
- battery warranty claim preparation
- pre-road-trip range planning
- software/feature transfer questions

## Programming mix

For the first 90 days:

- **50% buyer files** — subscriber-driving model interest.
- **30% problem explainers** — search acquisition and evergreen authority.
- **20% current market/policy** — urgency and freshness.

After 90 days, use actual returning-viewer and subscriber-per-view data to adjust.

## Format

- Main videos: 8–13 minutes.
- Cadence: one high-quality long-form video weekly.
- Shorts: up to two original cutdowns per long video, only when the short teaches a complete idea; Shorts are not required.
- Voice: calm, skeptical, practical, non-partisan, no mockery of buyers or owners.
- Structure:
  1. costly decision hook;
  2. verdict preview;
  3. evidence method;
  4. three to five decisive checks;
  5. unknowns;
  6. conditional verdict;
  7. next-video bridge.

## Differentiation rules

1. Never say “best” without a defined use case and comparison set.
2. Never state a price without date, location, trim, mileage and fee scope.
3. Never treat displayed range as state of health. [S09]
4. Never treat a recall lookup as complete service history. [S04]
5. Never apply a current warranty page blindly to an older VIN. [S06]
6. Never let a sponsor influence the verdict.
7. Never use a clip merely because it is visually interesting.
8. Every episode must contain at least one original decision tool: scorecard, calculator, matrix, map, timeline or checklist.

## Brand trust architecture

Each upload description contains:

- “Last verified” date;
- source links grouped as official / market / manufacturer;
- assumptions used in calculations;
- correction method;
- affiliate disclosure when applicable;
- safety disclaimer: no high-voltage repair instructions and local professional inspection recommended where appropriate.

Each video includes a five-second source slate and an end-screen prompt to download/copy the checklist—not a generic “smash subscribe” plea.

## Defensibility

The moat is not access to footage. It is the accumulated system:

- a model-year research database;
- source hierarchy and corrections history;
- repeatable score methodology;
- dated listing samples;
- viewer-submitted questions categorized by decision stage;
- reusable but substantively different visual modules;
- credibility earned by saying “unknown” when evidence is insufficient.

## Explicit exclusions

- high-voltage battery teardown or repair instructions;
- advice to bypass safety systems;
- hidden-camera dealer content;
- copied crash, fire or scam clips;
- unverified owner anecdotes presented as prevalence;
- political advocacy unrelated to buyer decisions;
- daily EV news summaries;
- manufacturer rumor videos;
- guaranteed savings, resale values or battery life.

## Definition of a successful video

A video succeeds when a viewer can make a better decision, not merely when it receives views. Operationally, the channel tracks:

- click-through rate;
- first-30-second retention;
- average percentage viewed;
- subscribers per 1,000 views;
- returning viewers;
- comments that name a next action (“I checked the VIN,” “I got the insurance quote,” “I walked away,” “I negotiated”).
